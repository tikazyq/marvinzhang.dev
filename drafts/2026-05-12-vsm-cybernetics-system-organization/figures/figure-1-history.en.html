Figure 01 — A short history of cybernetics

From feedback to viable systems, 1948–1985

Four works, four decades. Each one extends what the previous one couldn't quite reach: feedback → how much control you need → what shape the control must take → how it lives at organisational scale.
1948
Norbert Wiener
Cybernetics: Or Control and Communication in the Animal and the Machine
Feedback as a primitive. The same mathematics describes a thermostat, a pancreas, a market, an organisation.
"Control and communication" become a single subject — across animal and machine.
1956
W. Ross Ashby
An Introduction to Cybernetics
Law of Requisite Variety. A controller's variety must be at least the variety of what it controls — or there exist disturbances it cannot absorb.
"Only variety can destroy variety." The quantitative floor.
1970
Roger Conant & W. Ross Ashby
Every Good Regulator of a System Must Be a Model of That System
The model theorem. A controller must contain internal structure that mirrors the system it controls. Ashby said how big; this says what shape.
Lookup tables aren't enough. The regulator has to be a model.
1972
& 1985
Stafford Beer
Brain of the Firm (1972) · Diagnosing the System for Organizations (1985)
Viable System Model. Five recursive subsystems + audit + alarm. Cybernetics at organisational scale.
Not a blueprint to copy — a mirror to diagnose.
The unfinished thread. Cybernetics asks how a system preserves its identity under disturbance. Software architecture, for the last two decades, has been asking something else: how is the code partitioned? AI agent systems make the first question impossible to keep avoiding.
