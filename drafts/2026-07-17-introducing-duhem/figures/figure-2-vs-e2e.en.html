FIG 02 — RELATION TO OTHER TESTING TOOLS

The difference isn’t the engine — it’s the judgment structure

Classic e2e frameworks, AI testing frameworks like Midscene, and Duhem sit at different layers: what you write, what it anchors to — and above all, who judges.
e2e frameworks like Playwright
AI testing frameworks like Midscene
Duhem
What you write
A test program you maintain forever: async, waits, retries
Natural-language cases, interpreted by a model at run time
Acceptance criteria + derived declarative checks (YAML)
Anchored to
Implementation detail — selectors, timing; refactors break it
Page semantics — model-understood; refactor-tolerant, model-dependent
Intent — what “done” should look like
Mocks
Commonly paired with mocks and fixtures
Usually drives the real interface
No mocks — only the real deliverable
Who judges
Assertions in code, maintained by the writer — often the AI itself
Model-judged assertions (aiAssert) — probabilistic verdicts
Deterministic verdict, independent of the AI; red blocks release
Best position
Functional testing and debugging in the inner loop
Exploratory UI automation, case generation
The final acceptance gate before merge and release
Judgment structure is the dividing line: assertions handed to code, to a model, or to an independent mechanical judge. Duhem welcomes AI drafting the criteria but keeps models out of the verdict — uncertainty must not enter it.
