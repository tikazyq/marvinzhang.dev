On March 11, 2025, Anders Hejlsberg published a post titled [A 10x Faster TypeScript](https://devblogs.microsoft.com/typescript/typescript-native-port/): Microsoft was porting the TypeScript compiler to Go.

For most readers this was performance news. For me it was three old blog posts colliding. In 2021 I wrote, within the space of a few months, a piece asking [whether Go was really a panacea for backend development](/blog/golang), a piece arguing that [TypeScript was indispensable for large frontend projects](/blog/typescript), and a piece praising [C#'s developer experience](/blog/csharp) — where I pointed out, almost as trivia, that TypeScript and C# share the same creator. Five years later, all three protagonists converged in a single repository: the compiler of the language I called indispensable, rewritten in the language I interrogated, led by the man who fathered both TypeScript and C#. I knew those articles were connected. I did not expect the connection to close like this.

The collision is worth more than nostalgia, because it exposes what has changed underneath the argument. In 2021, we debated which language was better *for humans*: which syntax was cleaner, which learning curve was gentler, which type system annoyed you least. The debate never ended. But the referee changed. Since then, AI coding agents have become some of the highest-frequency authors — and the very first readers — of production code. An agent does not care that Go's error handling is tedious or that TypeScript's annotations are verbose. It cares about two things: **how fast it gets feedback**, and **whether that feedback can be trusted**.

That observation is the candidate thesis of this article, stated deliberately in its modest form: when agents join the authorship of your codebase, two criteria get promoted to first class in web stack selection — **agent loop latency** (how long one edit-to-trusted-feedback cycle takes) and **the density of machine-decidable verification signals** (how much of your correctness can be checked automatically, deterministically, per cycle). These criteria do not replace the human ones. They re-rank them. And under the new ranking, some of 2021's verdicts get crowned while others get overturned.

To make that case honestly, I will first excavate what we actually argued about in 2021, then define the two new criteria precisely, then examine three bodies of evidence from 2025–2026, then give the strongest counterarguments their own section, and finally propose a small selection framework you can disagree with productively.

## What We Argued About in 2021

Rereading your own five-year-old technical writing is a peculiar form of archaeology. The artifacts are familiar; the assumptions buried under them are what surprise you.

My [TypeScript article](/blog/typescript) (February 2021) made one argument in several costumes: types are a collaboration contract. JavaScript's freedom was the villain — as the folk saying I quoted put it, dynamic typing feels great right up until the refactoring becomes a cremation. The core mechanism I praised was error front-loading: a violated contract surfaces at compile time as a deterministic message, instead of surfacing in production as `undefined is not a function`. I framed this entirely as a benefit for human teams — interfaces as documentation, autocomplete as comfort.

My [frontend engineering article](/blog/frontend-engineering) (March 2021) was, in hindsight, a complaint dressed as a curriculum. It cataloged what a "real" frontend engineer had to master — Webpack, Babel, Node, NPM, Yarn, ESLint, TypeScript configs — and admitted that even a modestly sized project dragged half a gigabyte of dependencies behind it. The article's closing line asked, half-jokingly, whether anyone entering frontend could still keep up with the learning. The toolchain's complexity was treated as weather: something to be endured and mastered, not questioned.

My [Golang article](/blog/golang) (March 2021) refused to crown anything. Its method was explicitly dialectical: a language's most celebrated features become its double-edged swords. Simple syntax limits expressiveness; mandatory error handling produces `err` everywhere; compile speed, I joked in a pros-and-cons table, was the one row with no downside ("how could this possibly be a con?"). The article even made a falsifiable prediction — that generics would likely arrive in Go 2.0. Reality was kinder: [Go 1.18 shipped generics](https://go.dev/blog/go1.18) in March 2022, no version bump required.

My [C# article](/blog/csharp) (November 2021) introduced the frame I now find most durable: the gap between satisfaction and market share. Citing StackOverflow's 2021 survey of 82,914 developers, I noted that Java — the market's colossus — was loved by only 47% of its users, while C#, its supposed clone, scored 62%. My reading then: satisfaction is a leading indicator; market share is a lagging one. The article was titled "Part 1." Part 2 never appeared. Hold that thought.

![Four 2021 articles side by side — the criterion each argued from, its verdict then, and how it reads in 2026; every criterion denominated in humans](/img/blog/2026-07-06-web-stack-selection-in-the-agent-era/figure-1-2021-criteria.en.png)

Lined up this way, the four articles share an assumption so universal in 2021 that none of us thought to state it: every criterion was denominated in humans. Human learning curves, human reading comfort, human satisfaction surveys. Nobody asked how a machine would experience the stack, because no machine was writing the code.

## The Referee Has Changed

Watch what an agent actually does after it edits your code. It runs the type checker. It runs the tests. It reads the errors. It edits again. Depending on the harness, this loop closes dozens or hundreds of times per task — where a human developer might close it dozens of times per *day*. Two properties of your stack now dominate the agent's throughput, and neither appeared in my 2021 criteria.

**Loop latency** is the end-to-end time of one cycle: install, compile, type-check, test startup, feedback. For a human, a 70-second type check is an annoyance to schedule coffee around. For an agent iterating hundreds of times, latency *is* throughput — it multiplies into everything. The joke row in my 2021 Golang table, where compile speed was the only entry with no conceivable downside, has quietly become a first-order selection criterion.

**Verification signal density** is the amount of correctness per cycle that a machine can decide without judgment. A `tsc` error is free to produce, deterministic, and machine-readable: the same defect yields the same message every time, pointing at a file and a line. A runtime `TypeError` in production is the opposite: expensive to reach, stochastic to reproduce, and demanding human interpretation. The distinction matters more than it used to, because agents consume these signals mechanically. A 2025 study of LLM-generated code, [cited by GitHub's engineering blog](https://github.blog/ai-and-ml/llms/why-ai-is-pushing-developers-toward-typed-languages/), found that **94% of compiler errors in LLM output are type-check failures** — which means a type system is, empirically, the densest verification-signal generator an agent encounters.

![The agent loop: edit, then build/type-check/test, then a signal branch — deterministic machine-readable signals let the agent self-correct and loop back hundreds of rounds; weak runtime-only signals force a human to intervene. The stack sets the loop latency and the signal density.](/img/blog/2026-07-06-web-stack-selection-in-the-agent-era/figure-2-agent-loop.en.png)

One sentence carries the mechanical heart of this article: **training data determines how well an agent writes on the first attempt; verification signals determine how fast it converges on correct.** Agent workflows are multi-round by construction, so the convergence variable — not the first-draft variable — dominates the economics. I will stress-test that sentence in the counterarguments section, because it is the load-bearing wall.

I should also mark where this argument sits among its neighbors, because parts of it are not new. Daniel Demmel's [feedback loop engineering](https://www.danieldemmel.me/blog/feedback-loop-engineering) develops the loop-quality idea thoroughly but stays deliberately tool-agnostic — it refuses to talk stack selection. Netlify's [AX (agent experience)](https://biilmann.blog/articles/introducing-ax/) names the goal migration but aims it at platforms serving other people's agents. What I have not seen done is connecting both to the decision my 2021 articles were actually about — *which stack do you pick* — under a constraint neither discusses: the codebase must remain reviewable by humans while being loopable by agents. Those two demands conflict. Explicitness and verbosity help the agent and tax the human reader. "Joint DX" is not a slogan; it is a trade-off the framework at the end has to hold.

## The Verdicts Are Being Rewritten

Three bodies of evidence from 2025–2026, each with a direct echo in a 2021 article.

**Exhibit one: typescript-go.** The port announced in March 2025 reached [release candidate in June 2026](https://devblogs.microsoft.com/typescript/announcing-typescript-7-0-rc/) (`typescript@rc`; as I write, the stable release is still pending and npm's `latest` remains 6.0.3). Microsoft's own December 2025 measurements put the speedup at 7.5–10.2x on real codebases — the team now says "often about 10 times faster," a formulation more careful than the launch headline. Read the decision through the two criteria and it stops looking like a mere optimization: one of the most heavily funded compiler teams in the industry spent years porting a working compiler to buy *the same signal density at a tenth of the latency*. Both variables of the thesis, priced by a single engineering decision. The echoes are almost too neat. My TypeScript article's error-front-loading argument gets promoted from human convenience to machine feedstock. My Golang article's one joke row becomes the purchase's entire rationale. And Hejlsberg choosing Go over his own C# — [his stated reasons](https://github.com/microsoft/typescript-go/discussions/411) were portability of the existing code's style, not language allegiance — is itself a small demonstration that the criteria have changed: even the father of two languages picks by loop mechanics now.

**Exhibit two: Hono versus Express.** The [State of JS 2025 survey](https://2025.stateofjs.com/en-US/libraries/) put Hono in its S-tier — satisfaction above 90%, alongside Vite and Vitest — in its first year as a survey entry, while noting Express still leads usage "but the satisfaction metrics show its age." If that structure feels familiar, it is my C# article's chart with the names swapped: the market colossus that users tolerate, versus the newcomer that users love. Satisfaction leading, market share lagging — the 2021 frame transplants without modification. What makes Hono specifically interesting for this article's thesis, though, is *why* it satisfies. Its [RPC mode](https://hono.dev/docs/guides/rpc) shares server-side types directly with the client — no code generation, no drift-prone OpenAPI step — and its Zod validator integration is not a niche add-on: `@hono/zod-validator` alone pulls around 2.7 million weekly downloads on npm's registry API. In stack-selection terms, that is a framework turning the verification-signal argument into a full-stack contract: break the API shape on the server, and the client fails to *compile*.

```ts
// Server: route + validator define the contract
const app = new Hono().post('/users', zValidator('json', userSchema), handler)
export type AppType = typeof app

// Client: shapes checked by tsc — wrong body is a compile error, not a 500
const client = hc<AppType>('http://localhost:3000/')
```

A note on numbers, in the spirit of this article: Hono's raw download counts (around 47M/week on the registry's own API as I write) now place it second only to Express — but registry-wide figures have inflated dramatically in 2026 from CI and automation traffic, so I will only stand behind the relative claim: Hono's growth outpaces the inflated baseline, and its trajectory versus Express's is the satisfaction-versus-share gap in motion.

**Exhibit three: Bun, prosecution's half.** Bun's pitch is one binary that swallows the runtime, package manager, bundler, and test runner. Its install speed is the single most consistently replicated advantage across independent write-ups, and [State of JS 2025](https://www.infoq.com/news/2026/03/state-of-js-survey-2025/) has 21% of respondents using it (up 4 points year over year). The echo here is my frontend engineering article's central complaint — the 500MB dependency folders, the tool-list-as-curriculum. The complexity I treated as weather in 2021 turned out to be a market: Bun's entire narrative is collapsing that toolchain to zero. But I wrote the 2021 Golang article precisely to resist this kind of narrative, and the same dialectic applies with force here — which is why Bun's other half testifies for the defense in the next section.

![Three 2026 exhibits mapped to their 2021 echoes: typescript-go crowns the error-front-loading and compile-speed arguments; Hono vs Express reuses the satisfaction-versus-share frame; Hono RPC upgrades the type contract across the network; Bun answers the toolchain complaint — with a double edge](/img/blog/2026-07-06-web-stack-selection-in-the-agent-era/figure-3-verdicts.en.png)

## The Case Against

An argument this convenient deserves its strongest opposition. Three counterarguments, in descending order of threat.

**The training-data flywheel.** The [MultiPL-E benchmark](https://nuprl.github.io/MultiPL-E/) established that LLM code-generation accuracy correlates strongly with a language's training-corpus volume, and community discussions routinely cite lopsided spreads — one widely referenced benchmark has models near 97% correct in Elixir but only 61% in Rust. GitHub's own [Octoverse 2025](https://github.blog/news-insights/octoverse/typescript-python-and-the-ai-feedback-loop-changing-software-development/) describes the resulting flywheel: popular languages get better generation, which makes them more popular. If first-draft quality is what matters, the selection rule is simply "pick the popular one," and my two criteria are decoration. My response is the mechanism sentence from earlier: corpus size sets the *starting point* of each loop, verification density sets the *convergence rate*, and multi-round workflows compound the latter. But I must be honest about the evidentiary state: as of this writing there is no controlled study measuring end-to-end agent task success across stacks that isolates these variables. The thesis rests on mechanism and behavioral evidence, not on a verdict already delivered. That is why this article argues the modest form.

**Bun is its own counterexample.** Apply the 2021 Golang article's method — the table where every strength casts a shadow — to Bun, and the shadow is substantial:

![Bun scored by the 2021 Golang article method — every strength paired with its shadow: single binary vs compatibility surface and 5,000+ open issues; speed-first culture vs memory leaks and the OpenCode departure; AI-accelerated pace vs the production stability discount](/img/blog/2026-07-06-web-stack-selection-in-the-agent-era/figure-4-bun-double-edge.en.png)

The community's pragmatic consensus — use `bun install` for development speed, run Node in production — is itself a verdict: **loop-latency optimization cannot substitute for runtime reliability.** In the framework below, latency is one axis, not the whole plane. The most instructive Bun datapoint, though, cuts both ways. In June 2026, [Prisma moved its Compute product onto Bun's Rust-rewrite canary](https://www.prisma.io/blog/bun-rust-rewrite-prisma-compute) after finding that the *stable* release leaked memory and deadlocked connection pools while the canary passed their load tests. Their sentence deserves quoting: "The build which passed our tests made a better foundation than the build which failed them." A version label is a weak signal; a passed test suite is a decidable one. Even Bun's troubles end up illustrating the thesis.

**The scale itself is warped.** One more piece of honesty. The download counts we have always waved around in language debates — I did it in 2021 — are degrading as evidence. Through the first half of 2026, npm registry traffic roughly doubled across the board (Express's monthly downloads went from ~224M to ~460M with no plausible human explanation), widely attributed to CI and automated agents. The instrument we used to measure popularity now measures, in part, the machines. There is a certain justice in this: the era that demands harder verification signals is also the era in which our softest signal — the download counter — quietly stopped meaning anything.

## A Two-Axis Selection Framework

If the two criteria are real, they should be usable. Here is the plane they define, with placements that are qualitative judgments from the evidence above — not scores.

![A two-axis plane — loop latency versus verification signal density. TS 7 native, Go, and Hono + Zod sit in the joint-DX sweet spot; TS on the JS-based tsc is trustworthy but sluggish; plain JavaScript and typical Python setups are fast but sparse on signals; Bun today is furthest right with a reliability discount.](/img/blog/2026-07-06-web-stack-selection-in-the-agent-era/figure-5-quadrant.en.png)

The placements matter less than the rules for using them.

**First, the axes complement the entry requirements; they do not replace them.** Runtime reliability and ecosystem maturity remain non-negotiable tickets to the game — Bun's position on the latency axis buys it nothing in production until the reliability discount closes. No quadrant placement overrides a deadlocking connection pool.

**Second, "joint" means the human criteria keep their vote.** My 2021 Golang article's closing advice — weigh a technology's fit to the business, to the team, and to its learning and time costs — transfers intact; you simply run the assessment twice, once for your humans and once for your agents, and negotiate the conflicts explicitly. A codebase optimized into explicit, verbose, agent-legible form that your senior engineers dread reviewing has not achieved joint DX; it has swapped one audience's suffering for another's.

**Third, corpus adequacy is a precondition, not an axis.** The framework compares stacks *within* the well-trodden mainstream, where training data saturates. Choosing an exquisite but obscure language hands the agent a losing first draft that no amount of verification density fully repays — this is the legitimate core of the training-data objection, absorbed as a boundary condition.

What this framework refuses to produce is a sentence of the form "X is the best language for the agent era." I declined to write that sentence about Go in 2021 — any language that achieves broad adoption, I argued then, is not a bad language — and the intervening five years have only strengthened the refusal. Criteria travel; crowns do not.

## When the Subject Becomes the Object

One development from this research would have been beyond parody in 2021. Bun — the tool I have been weighing as a *choice for* agents — is now largely *built by* them. After Anthropic acquired the project in December 2025, the share of its commits authored by bots rose above 80% by [RedMonk's count](https://redmonk.com/sogrady/2026/06/04/bun-two-lessons/), and in May 2026 a million-line Rust rewrite, generated by coding agents over nine days, landed in a single merge. I cite this carefully: it is a showcase event by a vendor with every incentive to showcase, and one datapoint about one codebase. But as a signpost it is hard to improve on. The 2021 question — *which language is best for people to write* — has acquired a successor that the old debate had no grammar for: which stack best lets machines write, humans judge, and both trust the result.

That reframing answers the two loose ends I left in the archaeology. My frontend engineering article ended by asking whether anyone entering the field could still keep up with the learning. The 2026 answer is oddly gentle: you no longer have to learn all of it — the agent absorbed half the curriculum. What you cannot delegate is the judgment layer above it: deciding *by which criteria* your stack gets chosen, because the agent will happily optimize inside whatever loop you hand it. And my C# article's promised Part 2, five years overdue — consider this it. The sequel just turned out not to be about a language. It is about how the standards for choosing one quietly changed referees while we were busy arguing.
